A year ago I wanted something I couldn't have.

I'm being vague on purpose. Not because I'm protecting anyone, but because the specifics of what it was matter less than what I did about it. Or more accurately, what I didn't do about it.

Because historically? I know exactly what I would have done. I would have sat in it. Turned it over and over in my head like a rock tumbler that only makes things sharper. I would have replayed every interaction, rewritten every conversation, convinced myself that if I had just done *this one thing differently*, I'd have it. I would have spiraled. That's the word, right? Everyone uses it now. But when you're inside one, it doesn't feel like spiraling. It feels like thinking. It feels productive. It feels like you're working something out when really you're just wearing a groove deeper into the same spot.

I didn't do that this time.

I wish I could tell you there was a moment. Some clean before-and-after where I chose differently and everything shifted. But it wasn't like that. It was more like... I just got tired of the pattern. Tired of recognizing it. Tired of watching myself do the same thing and expecting it to feel different on the seventh or eighth lap.

So I did new things. I made myself uncomfortable.

I chose to accept that I might die at any point.

That sounds dramatic written down. But it's not. It's actually the least dramatic thing I've figured out. Everybody knows this intellectually. Nobody lives like it. I didn't either. I lived like I had infinite time to get things right, which meant I had infinite time to sit in the spiral and call it processing.

<!-- CAMERON: Was there a specific moment this clicked? Connected to the gun incident from essay 1? Or separate? -->

When you actually let that in (not as a concept, not as a motivational poster, but as a real thing that could happen on a Tuesday), it does something to the way you make decisions. Not in a reckless way. In a "why am I spending this hour ruminating about something I can't control" way.

I radically accepted my fate. Whatever it was going to be. The thing I wanted? Maybe I'd get it. Maybe I wouldn't. Both were fine. Not in a detached, zen, I-don't-care way. In a "I'm going to keep moving regardless" way.

And here's what happened. Not overnight. Over months.

The rumination slowed down. Not stopped. Slowed. The gap between something happening and me spiraling about it got wider. Wide enough that I could sometimes catch myself before the first lap. Wide enough that I could say "this is the pattern" and actually mean it, not just recognize it while doing it anyway.

I started to feel like a person who was building something instead of a person who was stuck.

Here's what I know now that I didn't a year ago: it's okay to feel sad. Not in the way people say it on Instagram with a sunset behind it. I mean it's okay to just... not be on. To have a Thursday where you're flat and not treat it like evidence that nothing has changed. To sit with a feeling without building a case around it.

I spent years treating sadness like a symptom. Like if I was sad, something was wrong. Something needed fixing. A medication needed adjusting. A therapist needed calling. A spiral needed starting. But sometimes you're just sad. Sometimes that's Tuesday. And Wednesday is different.

The expectation that you should feel good all the time is one of the most destructive things I ever internalized. Because every time I didn't feel good, I thought I was failing. And that thought was usually the thing that started the spiral. Not the sadness itself. The judgment of the sadness.

Breaking a cycle isn't dramatic. Nobody claps. You don't feel fundamentally different on the day it happens because you don't know it's happening. You just notice one day that the thing you used to do... you didn't do. And you didn't not-do it through willpower or discipline or some technique you learned. You just... moved past the spot where you used to get stuck.

[NEEDS LANDING]

I'm not fixed. I want to be clear about that. I don't even think "fixed" is a place you arrive at. But I'm not where I was a year ago. And the distance between those two points wasn't covered by thinking harder. It was covered by doing different things, feeling like shit sometimes, and not treating that as proof that I was broken.

For now, that's enough.
