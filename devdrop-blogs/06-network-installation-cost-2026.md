# How Much Does Commercial Network Installation Cost in 2026?

If you are planning a new office buildout, moving to a larger space, or finally replacing that network your business has been limping along on for the past decade, the first question is always the same: *how much is this going to cost?*

We get it. You need a number before you can budget, and the IT industry has not always been great about giving straight answers. Too many companies dodge the question, give vague ranges, or hit you with surprise charges after the work is done.

This guide gives you honest, transparent cost information for commercial network installation in 2026. These are industry-wide ranges -- not DevDrop-specific pricing -- but they will give you a solid foundation for planning your project and having informed conversations with any IT infrastructure provider.

## The Short Answer

For most small and mid-sized businesses (10 to 100 employees), a complete commercial network installation typically falls in the range of **$3,000 to $15,000**. That includes hardware, cabling, configuration, and installation labor.

Where you land in that range depends on several factors we will break down below: the size of your space, how many people and devices need to connect, whether you need new cabling, and what level of security and redundancy your business requires.

Some larger or more complex projects -- multi-building campuses, healthcare facilities with strict compliance requirements, or full warehouse coverage -- can run higher. But the $3,000 to $15,000 range covers the majority of SMB projects we see across West Michigan.

## Cost Breakdown by Component

Let us walk through every major component of a typical commercial network installation, what it does, and what it costs.

### Router

**Cost: $100 - $400**

The router is the gateway between your internal network and the internet. It directs traffic, assigns IP addresses, and serves as the starting point for your entire network.

For a small office, a quality business-grade router in the $100 to $200 range gets the job done. Larger offices or businesses with higher bandwidth needs may need a more capable unit in the $200 to $400 range. Some businesses combine the router function with their firewall appliance, which can simplify the setup.

### Network Switch

**Cost: $80 - $600**

A network switch connects all the wired devices in your office -- computers, printers, phones, security cameras, access points. Think of it as the central hub that everything plugs into.

The cost depends on the number of ports you need and whether you need managed or unmanaged:

| Switch Type | Port Count | Typical Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Unmanaged (basic) | 8-16 ports | $80 - $150 |
| Managed (configurable) | 24 ports | $200 - $400 |
| Managed with PoE | 24-48 ports | $300 - $600 |

**PoE (Power over Ethernet)** switches are worth calling out. They deliver power through the network cable itself, which means devices like wireless access points and security cameras do not need separate power outlets. For most business installations, a PoE switch saves money on electrical work and simplifies the overall setup.

### Structured Cabling

**Cost: $200 - $1,000+**

This is the physical wiring that connects everything -- the network drops running from each desk, office, or workspace back to your network closet or server room.

Structured cabling costs are driven by:

- **Number of drops:** Each cable run from a wall jack to the patch panel is called a "drop." A typical office needs one to two drops per workspace.
- **Cable type:** Cat6 is the current standard for most business installations. Cat6A supports higher speeds and is worth considering if you are planning for the long term or have heavy data needs.
- **Run length:** Longer runs from the network closet to distant offices or floors cost more in both materials and labor.
- **Conduit and pathway:** Whether cables run through existing pathways, above a drop ceiling, or need new conduit installed.

| Cable Type | Speed | Typical Use | Cost per Drop (installed) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cat6 | Up to 10 Gbps (short runs) | Standard office, most businesses | $150 - $250 |
| Cat6A | 10 Gbps (full 100m) | Healthcare, data-heavy environments | $200 - $350 |

A 10-person office might need 15 to 25 drops. A 50-person office could need 60 to 100+. The cabling is often the single largest line item in a network installation -- but it is also the component that lasts the longest. A properly installed Cat6 or Cat6A cabling system will serve your business for 15 to 20 years.

### Firewall

**Cost: $200 - $1,000**

Your firewall is the security boundary between your internal network and the outside world. It inspects traffic, blocks threats, enforces access policies, and -- for many industries -- is required for regulatory compliance.

| Firewall Tier | Typical Users | Cost Range |
|---|---|---|
| Entry-level business | 5-25 users | $200 - $400 |
| Mid-range (NGFW) | 25-100 users | $400 - $700 |
| Advanced (high throughput, full UTM) | 100+ users | $700 - $1,000+ |

Keep in mind that many enterprise-grade firewalls require an annual subscription for threat intelligence updates, content filtering, and vendor support. These subscriptions typically run $200 to $500 per year and are well worth it -- a firewall without current threat data is significantly less effective.

For a deeper look at what firewalls do and how to choose the right one, see our [Complete Guide to Business Firewall and VPN Security](/blog/business-firewall-vpn-guide).

### Wireless Access Points

**Cost: $100 - $300 each**

Wireless access points (APs) provide WiFi coverage throughout your space. The number you need depends on the size of your facility, building materials (concrete and metal block signal more than drywall), and how many wireless devices need to connect.

| Coverage Scenario | Access Points Needed | Total Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Small office (1,500-3,000 sq ft) | 1-2 | $100 - $600 |
| Medium office (3,000-8,000 sq ft) | 2-4 | $200 - $1,200 |
| Large office or warehouse (8,000+ sq ft) | 4-8+ | $400 - $2,400+ |

Business-grade access points from brands like Ubiquiti (UniFi) and Ruckus are designed to handle dozens of simultaneous connections without slowing down -- a major difference from consumer WiFi routers that start struggling after 15 to 20 devices.

For warehouses, manufacturing floors, and outdoor areas, you may need ruggedized or outdoor-rated access points, which cost slightly more but are built to handle dust, temperature swings, and moisture.

### Network Attached Storage (NAS)

**Cost: $300 - $1,500**

A NAS device provides centralized file storage that everyone on the network can access. It is essentially a dedicated file server -- a secure place to store and share documents, project files, backups, and media.

| NAS Type | Capacity | Best For | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2-bay (entry) | 2-8 TB | Small office, basic file sharing | $300 - $500 |
| 4-bay (mid-range) | 4-32 TB | Growing businesses, media storage | $500 - $900 |
| 6-8 bay (advanced) | 8-80+ TB | Heavy storage, redundancy, backups | $900 - $1,500 |

Not every business needs a NAS -- if you are fully cloud-based (Google Drive, SharePoint, Dropbox), you may not need on-site storage. But for businesses that handle large files, need fast local access, or want to keep sensitive data on-premise, a NAS is a smart investment.

### Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS)

**Cost: $75 - $300**

A UPS is a battery backup that keeps your network equipment running during power outages. When the power goes out, your UPS gives you enough time to either ride out a short outage or shut everything down gracefully -- preventing data loss, file corruption, and hardware damage.

| UPS Size | Runtime | Best For | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| 600-900 VA | 5-10 min | Single switch or router | $75 - $125 |
| 1000-1500 VA | 10-20 min | Small network closet | $125 - $200 |
| 2000-3000 VA | 15-30 min | Full rack, server room | $200 - $300+ |

We recommend a UPS for every network closet. The cost is minimal compared to the potential damage from an unexpected power loss -- especially if you have a server, NAS, or security camera system that needs to stay online.

### Server

**Cost: $500 - $5,000+**

Not every business needs a physical server, but many do. Common use cases include running line-of-business applications, hosting databases, managing Active Directory (user accounts and permissions), running security camera recording software, or handling specialized industry software that requires a local server.

| Server Type | Best For | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Refurbished tower server | Small office, basic file/print | $500 - $1,500 |
| New tower server | Small-to-mid business, general purpose | $1,500 - $3,000 |
| Rack-mounted server | Growing businesses, data center setups | $2,000 - $5,000+ |

Cloud services have reduced the need for on-premise servers for many businesses, but they have not eliminated it. If you are running a security camera system with local recording, hosting a specialized application, or operating in an industry with data residency requirements, a local server is still the right call.

## Factors That Affect Your Total Cost

Every business and building is different. Here are the main variables that push costs up or down:

### Building Type and Condition

A modern office building with drop ceilings and accessible cable pathways is significantly easier (and cheaper) to cable than an older building with solid walls, limited ceiling access, or no existing infrastructure. Open-plan offices are generally less expensive than buildings with many enclosed rooms.

### New Construction vs. Retrofit

Installing network infrastructure during new construction or a major renovation is always less expensive than retrofitting an existing space. When the walls are open and the ceilings are accessible, cabling is straightforward. **Retrofitting an existing building typically adds 25 to 40 percent to the cabling portion of the project** due to the additional labor required to route cables through finished walls and ceilings.

If you are planning a renovation or buildout, make sure your IT infrastructure is part of the conversation from day one -- not an afterthought after the drywall is up.

### Number of Drops and Devices

More employees means more network drops, more access points, potentially larger switches, and a more capable firewall. A 10-person office with 20 drops is a fundamentally different project than a 75-person office with 150 drops across two floors.

### Compliance Requirements

If your business falls under HIPAA (healthcare), PCI DSS (credit card processing), or other regulatory frameworks, your network needs additional security measures: network segmentation, advanced firewall configurations, encrypted connections, and detailed logging. These requirements do not necessarily add massive cost, but they do require more planning, configuration, and testing.

### Redundancy and Uptime Needs

If network downtime directly costs your business money -- which it does for most companies -- investing in redundancy pays for itself. This might include dual internet connections with automatic failover, high-availability firewall pairs, redundant switches, or UPS battery backup with extended runtime.

### Outdoor or Specialized Coverage

Warehouses, parking lots, loading docks, construction yards, and other outdoor areas require weatherproof equipment and longer cable runs. Manufacturing floors with heavy machinery, metal structures, or extreme temperatures may need ruggedized networking equipment.

## Ongoing Costs: What to Budget After Installation

Your network is not a one-time expense. Plan for these recurring costs:

| Ongoing Cost | Annual Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Internet service | $600 - $2,400 | Business-grade internet from a local ISP |
| Firewall subscriptions | $200 - $500 | Threat updates, content filtering, support |
| Software licenses | $100 - $500 | Network management, monitoring tools |
| Managed support or service agreement | $600 - $2,500 | Troubleshooting, maintenance, updates |
| Equipment replacement reserve | $200 - $600 | Budget for eventual hardware refreshes |

**Total estimated ongoing cost: $1,500 - $4,000 per year** for most small and mid-sized businesses.

Some of these costs are optional -- you do not *have* to have a managed support agreement, for example. But in our experience, businesses that invest in ongoing support and maintenance have fewer outages, fewer emergency repair bills, and networks that last significantly longer.

## Project Timelines

How long does a commercial network installation take? It depends on the scope:

| Project Size | Typical Timeline |
|---|---|
| Small office (5-15 employees, single room/suite) | 1-2 weeks |
| Medium office (15-50 employees, full floor) | 2-3 weeks |
| Large office or multi-floor (50-100+ employees) | 3-4 weeks |
| Multi-building or campus | 4-6+ weeks |

These timelines include the site survey, proposal, equipment procurement, installation, configuration, testing, and handoff. Most of the elapsed time is in planning and procurement -- the actual on-site installation work for a small to medium office is typically two to five days.

## How to Get an Accurate Quote

Online cost guides like this one are useful for ballpark planning, but every business and building is different. The only way to get a truly accurate number is to have a qualified IT infrastructure company visit your site, understand your needs, and put together a detailed proposal.

Here is what to look for in a good proposal:

- **Itemized hardware costs** -- you should see every piece of equipment listed with make, model, and price
- **Labor costs clearly separated** from hardware costs
- **A scope of work** that describes exactly what will be installed, configured, and tested
- **A timeline** with a clear start date and expected completion date
- **Warranty and support terms** -- what happens if something does not work after installation?
- **No hidden fees** -- if the total on the proposal is not the total on the invoice, that is a red flag

Be cautious of any company that gives you a quote without visiting your site. A phone estimate might get you in the right ballpark, but a proper site survey is the only way to account for building-specific factors like cable pathway access, wall construction, electrical capacity, and existing infrastructure.
