# How to Plan a Network for a New Office or Building

Moving into a new office or building is exciting. You're thinking about floor plans, furniture, signage, maybe even the break room coffee machine. But there's one thing that often gets pushed to the bottom of the list -- and it shouldn't: **your network.**

Email, phone systems, security cameras, point-of-sale terminals, cloud applications, file sharing -- all of it runs on your network. If you wait until after construction to think about it, you're setting yourself up for headaches, workarounds, and expenses that could have been avoided.

We've designed and installed networks for over 500 projects across West Michigan. Here's what we've learned about getting office network planning right from the start.

## Start Planning Before Construction, Not After

**Network planning should begin at the same time as your architectural and construction planning -- not after the drywall is up.**

Your network depends on physical infrastructure. Cable pathways need to be routed through walls, ceilings, and floors. Your server room needs dedicated space, power, and cooling. Wireless access points need to be positioned based on the layout of your space.

When you plan alongside construction, cabling runs cleanly through walls before they're closed up. Conduit gets placed during the rough-in phase. Electrical outlets get positioned exactly where your equipment will live. Everything is cleaner, faster, and less expensive.

When you plan after construction? You're looking at surface-mounted cable runs, cutting into finished walls, and compromises that affect both aesthetics and performance.

**The bottom line:** If you're signing a lease, buying a building, or starting a buildout, your IT infrastructure partner should be involved from day one.

## Assess Your Current and Future Needs

Before anyone draws up a network design, you need to understand what your network actually needs to support -- not just today, but three to five years from now.

### Count Your Users and Devices

How many people will work in this space? Each employee typically has two or three devices on the network: a computer, a phone, and maybe a tablet. Then add shared devices: printers, conference room displays, security cameras, access control systems, VoIP phones, and IoT devices like smart thermostats.

A 30-person office might easily have 100 or more devices on the network. You need to plan for that volume -- and then some.

### Plan for Growth

If you're moving into a new space, chances are your business is growing. Don't design a network for exactly what you need today. **Design for where you'll be in three to five years.**

That means running extra cable drops to offices and workstations even if they're not all occupied yet. It means installing a switch with room for expansion. It means making sure your server room can accommodate additional equipment down the road.

Adding cable runs and network capacity during initial construction costs a fraction of what it costs to retrofit later. Future-proofing isn't a luxury -- it's smart planning.

### Consider Bandwidth Requirements

Are you transferring large files? Running cloud-based applications? Hosting video conferences? Using VoIP phone systems? Each of these has different bandwidth demands. A professional IT installer can help you calculate the throughput you'll need and make sure your infrastructure can deliver it.

## Wired vs. Wireless: You Need Both

One of the most common questions we hear is: "Can we just go all wireless?" The short answer is no -- not if you want a reliable, high-performance network.

### Why Wired Still Matters

Wired connections (Ethernet) are faster, more stable, and more secure than wireless. For workstations, VoIP phones, security cameras, and printers, a wired connection is always the better choice. Wired connections also don't compete for bandwidth the way wireless devices do.

### Where Wireless Shines

Wireless is essential for mobility -- laptops in meeting rooms, tablets on the warehouse floor, guest devices in the lobby. The key is designing it properly: the right number of access points, placed in the right locations, configured for the number of devices in each area. A single consumer-grade router in the corner won't cut it.

### The Right Approach

**Use wired connections for stationary devices. Use wireless for everything that moves.** Your network design should include both, working together as a unified system.

## Where to Put the Server Room or Network Closet

Every office network needs a central hub where your switches, patch panels, firewall, UPS (battery backup), and potentially a server all live.

### Location Considerations

- **Central location:** Keep it central to your space. Shorter cable runs mean better performance and lower cost.
- **Dedicated space:** Don't share this room with your water heater or HVAC unit. Dust, heat, and moisture are the enemies of electronics.
- **Accessible but secure:** Your IT provider needs easy access for maintenance, but the room should be locked to prevent unauthorized access.

### Size It Right

A common mistake is making the network closet too small. Plan for at least a standard 12U to 24U rack with 36 inches of clearance in front. A closet that feels roomy today can feel cramped after you add a second switch, a UPS, and a camera recorder in year two.

## Cable Run Planning

Structured cabling is the physical wiring that connects every device in your building back to your network closet.

### Cable Type

For most business installations in 2026, **Cat6A** is the standard we recommend. It supports 10-gigabit speeds and provides better shielding against interference than older cable types. Cat6 is still acceptable for many applications, but Cat6A gives you headroom for future technology. Avoid Cat5e for new installations -- it won't support the speeds you'll need as your business grows.

### How Many Drops?

A "drop" is a single cable run from your network closet to a wall jack at a workstation or device location. The rule of thumb: **run more drops than you think you need.**

For a typical office workstation, plan for at least two drops -- one for the computer and one for a VoIP phone or other device. Conference rooms should have four to six drops. Common areas, break rooms, and reception desks should each have at least two.

Don't forget drops for security cameras, wireless access points, printers, and any other networked equipment.

### Labeling and Documentation

Every cable run should be labeled at both ends -- at the wall jack and at the patch panel in your network closet. Professional installers test and certify every run and provide documentation showing exactly what's connected where. At DevDrop, we live by a simple rule: **clean racks, clean results.**

## Power and Cooling

Network equipment generates heat, and it needs reliable power. These are two details that are easy to overlook and expensive to fix after the fact.

### Dedicated Electrical Circuits

Your network closet should have its own dedicated electrical circuit -- separate from the rest of the office. This prevents a tripped breaker from taking down your entire network.

### Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS)

A UPS provides battery backup during power outages. Even a brief power flicker can reboot switches and firewalls, disrupting your entire office. A properly sized UPS keeps everything running long enough for power to be restored or for a graceful shutdown.

### Cooling

A network closet with a switch, firewall, and UPS generates more heat than you'd expect. Without proper ventilation, equipment will overheat and eventually fail. For larger installations, a dedicated mini-split or in-rack cooling unit may be necessary. Your HVAC contractor and IT installer should coordinate on this during the buildout.

## Security Considerations

Your network is a gateway to your business data. Security should be built into the design from the beginning, not bolted on after the fact.

### Firewall

Every business network needs a commercial-grade firewall between your internal network and the internet. Consumer-grade routers don't provide the level of protection a business needs.

### Network Segmentation

Guest WiFi should be completely separate from your internal business network. Security cameras and IoT devices should be on their own segment. This limits the damage if any single device is compromised.

### Physical Security

Your network closet should be locked. Wireless access points should be mounted in locations that aren't easily tampered with. Physical access to your network equipment is a security concern that's often overlooked.

### Compliance

If you're in healthcare, finance, or any regulated industry, your network design needs to support compliance requirements. HIPAA, PCI-DSS, and other standards have specific rules about how data is transmitted and stored on your network.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

We've seen these mistakes hundreds of times. Every one of them is avoidable with proper planning.

### Under-Planning

Designing a network for exactly what you need today, with no room for growth. In two years, you'll be paying for retrofit work that costs three to four times what it would have cost to include in the original installation.

### Cheap Equipment

The router you bought at the electronics store for your home is not designed to support 50 devices in a commercial environment. Business-grade equipment is more reliable, more capable, and built to run 24/7.

### Skipping the Site Survey

Every space is different. Wall materials, ceiling heights, interference sources, and building construction all affect network performance. A proper site survey identifies these factors before installation begins.

### Not Involving Your IT Installer Early Enough

**Bring your IT infrastructure partner into the conversation before construction starts.** The earlier we're involved, the cleaner, faster, and less expensive the installation will be.

### Ignoring Cable Management

Messy cabling leads to troubleshooting nightmares, airflow problems in your rack, and accidental disconnections. Professional cable management is not cosmetic -- it's functional.

## Working With Your IT Installer During Construction

The best outcomes happen when your IT installer, general contractor, and electrician are all coordinating during the buildout. Here's what that looks like in practice.

### During the Design Phase

Your IT installer reviews the floor plan and creates a network design showing cable pathways, drop locations, access point placement, and network closet specifications. This design gets shared with your contractor so they can plan accordingly.

### During Rough-In

While walls are open, your IT installer or a coordinated low-voltage cabling crew runs all the cable. Conduit is placed for future runs. Backing boards or plywood are mounted in the network closet for rack installation.

### After Construction

Once the space is finished, your IT installer terminates all cable runs, installs wall jacks and patch panels, mounts and configures equipment, and tests everything end to end. You get a fully documented, certified network that's ready for your team to use on day one.
