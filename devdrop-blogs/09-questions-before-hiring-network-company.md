# 5 Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Network Installation Company

Your business network is the backbone of everything you do. Email, phones, security cameras, point-of-sale systems, cloud applications -- it all runs on your network. When it works, nobody thinks about it. When it doesn't, everything stops.

So when it's time to install a new network, upgrade an aging one, or build out a new office space, the company you hire matters. A lot.

The problem is that most business owners, office managers, and property managers aren't network engineers. You shouldn't have to be. But you do need to know enough to tell the difference between a company that will do the job right and one that will leave you with a mess to clean up six months later.

Here are five questions you should ask any network installation company before you sign a contract. These aren't trick questions. They're the basics that separate professional IT infrastructure companies from the ones that cut corners.

---

## 1. Do You Use Your Own Technicians or Do You Subcontract the Work?

This is the most important question on the list, and it's the one most people never think to ask.

### Why It Matters

A lot of IT companies -- even well-known ones -- don't actually do the installation work themselves. They sell you the project, then hand it off to a subcontractor you've never met. Sometimes that subcontractor hires their own subcontractor. By the time someone shows up at your building with a ladder and a box of cable, they may have no direct relationship with the company you're paying.

That creates problems. Communication breaks down. Accountability gets murky. If something goes wrong during the install or six months later, you're stuck in a game of phone tag between the company you hired and the crew that actually did the work. Neither one wants to take responsibility, and you're the one dealing with the fallout.

Subcontracting also means inconsistent quality. One crew might do excellent work. The next one might rush through the job because they're juggling three other projects. You have no way to know what you're getting until it's already done.

### What a Good Answer Looks Like

The company you hire should use their own in-house technicians for every project. That means the people who show up at your door are employees of the company, trained to their standards, and accountable to their leadership. If something goes wrong, there's one phone number to call and one company that owns the outcome.

## 2. Are You Licensed and Insured?

This one sounds obvious. It isn't. You'd be surprised how many businesses hire IT contractors without verifying this, and how many contractors operate without proper licensing or insurance.

### Why It Matters

Network installation is physical work. Technicians are pulling cable through walls, ceilings, and conduit. They're mounting equipment, drilling into structural elements, and working with electrical systems. If something goes wrong -- a worker gets injured on your property, a pipe gets hit during a cable pull, or equipment gets damaged -- you need to know you're protected.

If the company isn't insured, that liability could land on you. Your commercial property insurance might cover some of it, but you could still be looking at lawsuits, repair costs, and project delays. It's a risk that's completely avoidable.

Licensing matters too. Different states and municipalities have requirements for low-voltage cabling, electrical work, and network installation. A licensed company has met those requirements, which means they understand local building codes and industry standards. An unlicensed company is a gamble.

### What a Good Answer Looks Like

The answer should be a clear, immediate "yes" to both. A professional network installation company should be able to provide proof of insurance and licensing without hesitation. If they hedge, change the subject, or say they're "working on it," that's a red flag.

## 3. What Does Your Proposal Include -- and Will There Be Surprise Costs?

Few things are more frustrating than getting a bill that's significantly higher than the estimate you were given. It happens more often than you'd think in the IT infrastructure world, and it usually comes down to vague proposals.

### Why It Matters

Some companies give you a ballpark number over the phone, a one-page estimate with minimal detail, or a proposal that's full of fine print and exclusions. That leaves the door wide open for "change orders" and unexpected costs once the project is underway.

Maybe the original estimate didn't account for the cable runs being longer than expected. Maybe there's an extra charge for wall penetrations, or the equipment costs weren't clearly itemized. Maybe the permit fees weren't included. Each one of these surprises erodes your trust and blows up your budget.

A vague proposal also makes it impossible to compare companies fairly. If one company gives you a three-page proposal with itemized costs and another gives you a one-paragraph email with a total at the bottom, you're not comparing apples to apples.

### What a Good Answer Looks Like

A professional network installation company will conduct a site survey before giving you a price. They'll walk your space, assess the infrastructure, ask about your needs, and then deliver a detailed written proposal that covers everything: equipment, labor, materials, cable runs, mounting hardware, configuration, testing, and any permits or coordination required.

The proposal should be clear enough that you can read it and understand what you're paying for -- even if you're not a technical person. And the company should be upfront about what could cause the price to change, like discovering unexpected conditions behind a wall.

## 4. Do You Test and Document Everything Before Handoff?

This is where a lot of network installations fall apart -- not during the install, but after it. The cables are in the walls, the equipment is on the rack, and the crew leaves. Then you discover that a few cable runs don't work, the network map is a mystery, and nobody labeled anything.

### Why It Matters

Testing and documentation are what separate a professional installation from a hack job. Without proper testing, you won't know if a cable run has a fault until someone plugs in and nothing happens. Without documentation, your next IT provider (or your internal team) will have no idea what's connected to what, which ports go where, or how the network is configured.

Poor documentation creates ongoing costs. Every time you need to troubleshoot an issue, add a new workstation, or make a change, someone has to figure out the existing setup from scratch. That means more billable hours, longer downtime, and more frustration.

And it's not just the cables. Your firewall rules, VLAN configurations, wireless settings, and IP addressing should all be documented. If the only person who knows how your network is configured is the technician who installed it, you're one phone call away from being stranded if that person isn't available.

### What a Good Answer Looks Like

Every cable run should be labeled on both ends. Every run should be tested and certified to industry standards. The company should provide you with complete documentation that includes a network diagram, equipment list, port maps, configuration details, and test results.

Before the crew leaves your building, they should walk you through everything. You should understand how your network is set up, where the critical equipment is, and who to call if something needs attention.

## 5. What Happens After the Install -- Do You Offer Ongoing Support?

The install is only the beginning of your relationship with your network. Equipment needs firmware updates. Configurations need adjustments as your business changes. Things break. New employees need to be connected. You'll have questions.

### Why It Matters

A lot of IT companies treat installation as a one-time transaction. They do the work, hand you an invoice, and move on to the next job. When you call six months later because your wireless access points aren't performing like they used to, you get voicemail. Or you get told that support will be an additional charge that wasn't discussed upfront.

Your network is a living system. It needs attention over time. If the company that installed it doesn't offer ongoing support, you'll have to find another company to maintain it -- and that new company will have to reverse-engineer everything the first company did. That's expensive, time-consuming, and avoidable.

### What a Good Answer Looks Like

The company should clearly explain their post-installation support options. Do they offer a warranty period? Is there a support phone number or email? Do they provide service agreements for regular maintenance and priority response? Can they handle future upgrades and expansions?

The best network installation companies think of themselves as long-term partners, not one-time vendors. They want to be the first call you make when something needs attention, and they make it easy for you to reach them.
