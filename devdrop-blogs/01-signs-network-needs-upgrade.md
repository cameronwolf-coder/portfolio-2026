# 7 Signs Your Business Network Needs an Upgrade

Your business network is a lot like the plumbing in your building. When it works, nobody thinks about it. When it doesn't, everything grinds to a halt.

The problem is that networks don't usually fail all at once. They degrade slowly -- a dropped connection here, a sluggish file transfer there -- until one day your team is spending more time waiting on technology than actually getting work done.

After completing over 500 projects for more than 200 businesses across West Michigan, we've seen the same warning signs over and over again. Here are seven that tell you it's time to seriously consider a business network upgrade.

---

## 1. Your Internet Feels Painfully Slow (Even Though You're Paying for Fast Speeds)

This is the one we hear the most. A business owner calls and says, "We just upgraded our internet plan, but everything still feels slow." They're frustrated, and rightfully so. They're paying for speed they're not getting.

Here's what's usually going on: your internet service provider delivers bandwidth to your building, but your internal network -- the switches, routers, cabling, and access points inside your walls -- is the bottleneck. Think of it like hooking a fire hose up to a garden sprinkler. The water pressure is there, but the sprinkler can only push out so much.

**What slow speeds actually look like day to day:**

- Files take minutes to upload or download instead of seconds
- Cloud-based apps like QuickBooks Online, Microsoft 365, or your CRM lag constantly
- Your team avoids using shared drives because it's faster to just email files back and forth
- Simple web pages take noticeably longer to load than they do on your phone at home

If your internal network hardware hasn't been updated in several years, there's a good chance the equipment simply can't keep up with the speeds your ISP is delivering. Older switches might only support 100 Mbps when your plan delivers 500 Mbps or more. That's money you're spending every month on speed your team will never see.

**The fix:** A network assessment can pinpoint exactly where the bottleneck is -- whether it's aging switches, outdated cabling, or misconfigured equipment -- so you're not guessing.

## 2. Your WiFi Drops Out or Disconnects Randomly

Frequent disconnections are more than an annoyance. They interrupt video calls, kick people off shared applications, and force your team to waste time reconnecting, re-logging in, and figuring out where they left off.

Random drops usually point to one of a few issues:

- **Overloaded access points.** Consumer-grade routers are designed to handle a handful of devices. When you have 20, 30, or 50 devices all trying to connect to one or two access points, the hardware simply can't keep up.
- **Interference.** Microwaves, Bluetooth devices, neighboring businesses' WiFi networks, and even certain building materials can interfere with your wireless signal. Without proper channel planning, your access points may be fighting for airspace.
- **Outdated firmware or hardware.** Older equipment that hasn't been updated can develop stability issues over time. Manufacturers release firmware updates to fix bugs and improve performance, but if nobody's managing your network, those updates don't happen.

We've walked into offices where the entire company was running off a single consumer router from a big-box store. It worked fine when they had five employees. At twenty-five employees with laptops, phones, and tablets, it was falling apart daily.

**The fix:** Commercial-grade access points with proper placement, channel management, and load balancing are designed to handle dozens of simultaneous connections without breaking a sweat.

## 3. You Have WiFi Dead Zones in Your Building

You know the spots. The back warehouse where nobody can get a signal. The conference room where video calls drop every time. The break room where your team can't even check email on their phones.

WiFi dead zones happen when access points aren't placed strategically or when there simply aren't enough of them to cover your space. Building materials play a huge role, too. Concrete walls, metal studs, glass partitions, and even large pieces of equipment can block or weaken wireless signals.

**This matters more than you might think:**

- Warehouse and production floor staff can't use wireless scanners, tablets, or inventory management tools
- Employees migrate to the "good spots" instead of working at their desks
- Customer-facing areas like lobbies or waiting rooms have poor or no WiFi coverage
- Security cameras in certain areas can't maintain a reliable wireless connection

A proper wireless site survey -- where someone physically maps your space and tests signal strength throughout the building -- is the only reliable way to solve dead zone problems. No amount of "just moving the router" is going to fix a coverage issue caused by building layout and construction materials.

**The fix:** A professional wireless design with access points placed based on actual site survey data, not guesswork.

## 4. Your Network Equipment Is More Than 5 Years Old

Technology moves fast, and network hardware is no exception. If your switches, routers, firewalls, or access points are more than five years old, they're likely falling behind in three important areas:

**Speed.** Older equipment often can't support current connection speeds. A switch from 2019 might max out at 100 Mbps per port when today's standard is gigabit (1,000 Mbps) or faster.

**Security.** Manufacturers stop releasing security patches for older hardware. That means known vulnerabilities go unpatched, leaving your network exposed. This is especially concerning for businesses that handle sensitive client data, patient records, or financial information.

**Features.** Newer network hardware supports features like VLAN segmentation (keeping your guest WiFi completely separate from your business network), advanced traffic prioritization (so your video calls get bandwidth priority over someone streaming music), and centralized management (so your entire network can be monitored and managed from a single dashboard).

Here's a quick gut check: look at your network closet or server room. If you see equipment with faded labels, dust buildup, and blinking lights that nobody can explain, it's probably overdue for a refresh.

**The fix:** A planned hardware refresh -- done on your schedule, not in an emergency -- lets you budget for the upgrade and minimize disruption to your team.

## 5. You're Worried About Security (or You Haven't Thought About It at All)

Both of these situations are a problem, and honestly, the second one worries us more.

Cybersecurity threats targeting small and mid-sized businesses have increased dramatically in recent years. Ransomware, phishing, and unauthorized network access are no longer things that only happen to big corporations. Businesses with 10 to 100 employees are frequently targeted precisely because attackers know their defenses are often weaker.

**Warning signs your network security needs attention:**

- You don't have a business-grade firewall (or you're not sure what firewall you have)
- Your WiFi password hasn't changed in years, and former employees still know it
- Guest visitors connect to the same network as your business systems
- You have no VPN for remote workers accessing company resources
- Nobody is monitoring your network for unusual activity
- Your security cameras are on the same network as your computers with no segmentation

A properly designed network separates traffic so that a compromised device on your guest network can't reach your business systems. It uses a commercial firewall with active threat detection, automatic updates, and proper access controls. It ensures that only authorized devices and users can access sensitive resources.

**The fix:** A network security assessment identifies your vulnerabilities and prioritizes them so you can address the biggest risks first without having to overhaul everything at once.

## 6. You Can't Support New Devices, Employees, or Locations

Growth is good. But if your network wasn't designed to scale, adding new people and devices can push an already-strained system past its breaking point.

**Here's how this usually plays out:**

- You hire five new employees and suddenly the WiFi is noticeably slower for everyone
- You add a new VoIP phone system, and now call quality is terrible
- You install security cameras, and video playback is choppy and unreliable
- You open a second location and have no idea how to connect the two networks securely
- You roll out tablets or mobile devices for your team and the network can't handle the additional load

Every device on your network consumes bandwidth and requires an IP address, a connection to an access point, and a path through your switches and firewall. Networks designed for 20 devices don't magically handle 60 without upgrades to the underlying infrastructure.

The businesses we work with in manufacturing, healthcare, construction, and hospitality are constantly adding connected devices -- from production floor terminals to patient check-in tablets to point-of-sale systems. If the network foundation isn't built to scale, every addition makes the problem worse.

**The fix:** A scalable network design accounts for your current needs and your planned growth over the next three to five years, so you're not rebuilding from scratch every time you hire someone new.

## 7. Video Calls Are Choppy, Laggy, or Constantly Dropping

This one has become a deal-breaker for most businesses. Video conferencing isn't optional anymore. Whether you're using Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Meet, or another platform, your team needs reliable video calls to communicate with clients, vendors, remote employees, and partners.

Poor video call quality almost always traces back to a network issue, not a software issue. Here's why:

- **Insufficient bandwidth.** Video calls require consistent, sustained bandwidth -- both upload and download. If your network can't provide that, you get pixelated video and robotic audio.
- **No traffic prioritization.** On a basic network, a video call gets the same priority as someone downloading a large file or streaming music. A properly configured network uses Quality of Service (QoS) settings to prioritize real-time communication traffic.
- **WiFi instability.** If the person on the video call is connected via WiFi and the signal is weak or congested, the call suffers. Wired connections for conference rooms and key workstations make an enormous difference.
- **Jitter and latency.** These are technical terms for the delay and inconsistency in how data packets travel across your network. Older or overloaded equipment introduces jitter and latency, which directly degrades call quality.

When your sales team is presenting to a prospect and the call freezes mid-sentence, that's not just an inconvenience. It's a missed opportunity and a bad first impression.

**The fix:** Proper network design with QoS configuration, adequate bandwidth allocation, and reliable wireless coverage ensures that video calls work the way they should -- every time.
